I am a master's student in Journalism & Communication (Intelligent Communication) at Wuhan University (2024–2027), advised by Prof. Qingxing Dong. My research lies at the intersection of computational social science, complex systems, and network science.

Many of the questions that interest me share a common theme: how do large-scale patterns emerge from countless local interactions?

I am fascinated by phenomena such as information diffusion, collective behavior, and the dynamics of social and technological systems. What draws me to these problems is the possibility that seemingly complex outcomes may arise from relatively simple underlying mechanisms.

To explore these questions, I use computational approaches including network analysis, machine learning, and agent-based modeling. My broader goal is to better understand how complex adaptive systems evolve, respond to change, and generate emergent behavior.

I am actively seeking a PhD position starting Fall 2027 in computational social science, complex systems, or network science. If my research interests align with yours, I would be delighted to connect.

Previously, I interned as an algorithm research engineer at iFlytek Healthcare (May–Sep 2025), where I built NER systems, multi-task learning frameworks for food safety, and epidemiological models (SIR/MSEIR + ABM). I also interned at MiraclePlus (YC China, Feb–Jun 2023) as a product intern.

Outside of research, I enjoy table tennis — I've competed in several university tournaments — along with cycling, hiking, and reading.

Publications

  • How Misinformation Diffuses on Online Social Networks: Radical Opinions, Adaptive Relationship and Algorithmic Intervention
    Mengyi Zhang, Qingxing Dong, Xiaozhen Wu
    IEEE Transactions on Computational Social Systems (SCI Q1), 2025
    Abstract

    Designed a dynamic model to capture the co-evolution of users' misinformation beliefs and social relationships under algorithmic interventions. Introduced assimilating and repelling nodes to analyze their roles in opinion evolution. Integrated five recommendation algorithms (content-based, user-based, relationship-based, popularity-based, and scheduled) to simulate information distribution mechanisms. Conducted repeated experiments in Python to analyze recommendation ratios, algorithms, node types, and rewiring rules on opinion polarization and moderation.
